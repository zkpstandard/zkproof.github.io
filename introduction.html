Introduction

Basics and History

Zero Knowledge Proofs were first introduced by Goldwasser, Micali and Rackoff in the late 80's. In [GMR89], they created a new proving procedure with an efficient method for communicating a proof, or in modern terms, an interactive proof system with an efficient verifier. An interactive proof is an interactive process in which a prover probabilistically convinces a verifier of the correctness of a non-trivial mathematical proposition with overwhelming probability. In 2012, Shafi and Silvio received the A.M. Turing Award "for transformative work that laid the complexity-theoretic foundations for the science of cryptography".

Proving systems can be made zero knowledge, meaning the verifier can learn nothing but the correctness of the proposition, and hence allowing for increased privacy for the prover. Another line of research resulted in the study of non-interactive zero knowledge proofs, which is a variant of the above, except that there is no interaction between the prover and the verifier. These proving systems are commonly called Non-Interactive Zero Knowledge proofs (NIZK). Furthermore, a zk-SNARK is a variant of a non-interactive ZK proof system that enables a prover to generate a succinct proof to convince a very efficient verifier.

Standardization

This effort is the result of an growing interest from the industry, with major enterprise players betting on the technology to be made commercially viable, and from academia, with an increase in results and papers published on the subject. The efficiency of existing constructions have enabled the development of practical implementations and tools, and it has become clear that a common framework is needed for the proper development of this expanding field.
